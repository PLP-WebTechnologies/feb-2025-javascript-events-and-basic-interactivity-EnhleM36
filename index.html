Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

The Revolutionary DNA Amplification Technique

Introduction to PCR

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) is a revolutionary technique in molecular biology used to make millions to billions of copies of a specific DNA segment. Developed by Kary Mullis in 1983, this technique has transformed scientific research, medical diagnostics, forensic science, and many other fields.

How PCR Works

Denaturation (94-96°C)

The double-stranded DNA is heated to separate into two single strands. This happens because the hydrogen bonds between complementary bases are broken at high temperatures.

This step typically occurs at 94-96°C for 20-30 seconds. The high temperature breaks the hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs, separating the DNA double helix into two single-stranded DNA molecules.

Annealing (50-65°C)

The temperature is lowered so that the primers can bind (anneal) to their complementary sequences on the single-stranded DNA.

This step typically occurs at 50-65°C for 20-40 seconds. The exact temperature depends on the primers used - it must be low enough for specific hybridization between primers and template but high enough to prevent non-specific binding.

Extension (72°C)

DNA polymerase synthesizes a new DNA strand complementary to the template strand by adding dNTPs in the 5' to 3' direction.

This step typically occurs at 72°C, which is the optimal temperature for most DNA polymerases. The time needed depends on the length of the DNA fragment being amplified and the speed of the polymerase, but is typically 1 minute per 1000 base pairs.

Components Needed for PCR

+
  • Template DNA: The DNA containing the target sequence to be amplified
  • DNA Polymerase: Heat-stable enzyme (like Taq polymerase) that assembles new DNA strands
  • Primers: Short DNA fragments that provide a starting point for DNA synthesis
  • Nucleotides (dNTPs): The building blocks for the new DNA strands
  • Buffer Solution: Maintains optimal pH and ion concentrations for the reaction
  • Magnesium Ions: Required as a cofactor for DNA polymerase activity

Types of PCR

+
  • Real-Time PCR (qPCR): Monitors the amplification of targeted DNA sequence in real-time
  • Reverse Transcription PCR (RT-PCR): Converts RNA to DNA before amplification
  • Multiplex PCR: Uses multiple primers to amplify several targets simultaneously
  • Nested PCR: Increases specificity with two rounds of amplification using different primers
  • Digital PCR: Provides absolute quantification of target nucleic acids

Applications of PCR

PCR has revolutionized many fields due to its ability to produce millions of copies of a specific DNA sequence in a relatively short time. Click on the buttons below to learn about different applications:

Medical Diagnostics

PCR is widely used in medical diagnostics to detect infectious diseases, genetic disorders, and cancer markers.

  • Detection of bacterial and viral infections (including COVID-19)
  • Diagnosis of genetic diseases
  • Identification of cancer biomarkers
  • Monitoring of treatment effectiveness
  • Prenatal genetic screening

Forensic Science

PCR has revolutionized forensic science by allowing analysis of minuscule DNA samples.

  • DNA fingerprinting in criminal investigations
  • Paternity testing
  • Identification of human remains
  • Archaeological studies
  • Wildlife forensics to combat illegal trading of endangered species

Research Applications

PCR is a fundamental tool in molecular biology research.

  • Cloning experiments
  • Gene expression studies
  • Genetic engineering
  • Evolutionary biology and phylogenetic studies
  • Metagenomics and microbiome analysis

The History of PCR

PCR was invented by Kary MullisAmerican biochemist who was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1993 in 1983 while working for Cetus Corporation. Here's a timeline of key developments:

Discovery (1983)

While driving on Highway 128 from San Francisco to Mendocino, Kary Mullis conceived the idea of using repeated cycles of DNA polymerase activity and temperature changes to amplify DNA sequences. This insight would later earn him the 1993 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.

Early Development (1984-1988)

Early PCR was labor-intensive, requiring the addition of fresh polymerase after each heating step because the enzymes would denature. The discovery and isolation of Taq polymerase, a heat-stable enzyme from the bacterium Thermus aquaticus found in hot springs, revolutionized the process by eliminating this need.

Modern PCR (1989-Present)

The development of automated thermal cyclers in the late 1980s made PCR widely accessible. Since then, numerous variations have been developed, including real-time PCR, digital PCR, and highly multiplexed applications that have expanded its utility across many fields of science and medicine.

PCR Glossary

Key Terms

+
Denaturation
The process of separating double-stranded DNA into single strands by heating.
Annealing
The binding of primers to complementary sequences on the single-stranded DNA template.
Extension
The synthesis of new DNA strands by DNA polymerase.
Primer
Short sequences of nucleotides that serve as a starting point for DNA synthesis.
Taq Polymerase
A heat-stable DNA polymerase obtained from the bacterium Thermus aquaticus.
